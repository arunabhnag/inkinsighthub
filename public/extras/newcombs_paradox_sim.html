Newcomb's Paradox — Simulation Overview

The Paradox Statement

A supercomputer of near-perfect predictive accuracy has already examined you and sealed its prediction before you enter the room. You are shown two boxes:

Box A — transparent
$1,000
Always contains $1,000. Visible. Guaranteed.
Box B — sealed / opaque
$0 or $1,000,000
Contains $1M if the supercomputer predicted you would take only this box. Empty if it predicted you would take both.

You must choose: take only Box B (one-box), or take both boxes (two-box). The prediction is already made and cannot change.

The philosophical tension: Causal reasoning says always two-box — the prediction is fixed, so taking both can only add $1,000. Evidential reasoning says one-box — being the kind of person who one-boxes is evidence that Box B contains $1M. This simulation shows what happens empirically as the supercomputer's accuracy compounds over rounds.

Design Considerations & Choices
  • Participant types Three archetypes coexist: fixed one-boxers (always choose Box B only), fixed two-boxers (always take both), and adaptive participants who watch peer outcomes each round and probabilistically shift their lean — 15% chance of moving toward one-boxing if it outperformed, 10% toward two-boxing otherwise. This asymmetry reflects that switching away from a winning strategy is harder than being pulled toward one.
  • Per-participant learning The supercomputer maintains a separate accuracy score for each individual, initialized near the global baseline with a small random offset. It learns faster from correct predictions (full learning rate) than it unlearns from mistakes (40% of learning rate), modelling how real predictive systems accumulate confidence asymmetrically. Each person's history is the supercomputer's primary signal — it has genuinely "studied you."
  • Prediction timing Faithful to the paradox: the supercomputer commits its prediction before the participant's choice resolves. The learning update fires only after both are known. This enforces the causal independence that makes the paradox philosophically live — the prediction is already sealed when you choose.
  • Accuracy ceiling A configurable ceiling (default 98%) prevents the supercomputer from reaching 100%. At 100% accuracy the paradox collapses — one-boxing is trivially dominant. Near the ceiling, the paradox retains its philosophical bite: two-boxing still seems rational causally, yet one-boxers continue to outperform empirically.
  • Accuracy floor Individual SC accuracy is floored at 50% (random chance). A supercomputer cannot be systematically worse than random on a binary choice without exhibiting exploitable bias. Below 50%, two-boxing clearly dominates by pure expected value — the interesting region of the paradox lives above 67%.
  • Payout mechanics Two-boxers benefit directly from supercomputer errors: they always receive $1,000 from Box A, and collect the $1M from Box B precisely when the supercomputer wrongly predicted them as one-boxers. This means two-boxers are secretly rooting for SC mistakes, which is exactly the tension the paradox exploits.
  • Participant count Configurable from 500 to 50,000. Larger populations smooth statistical noise in the wealth curves and produce cleaner convergence on the accuracy chart. At lower counts (<2,000), round-by-round variance in adaptive participant behavior becomes visible as jitter in the wealth lines — a real and informative signal, not a bug.
  • What to watch The key inflection is around 75–80% SC accuracy. Below that, two-boxers can stay competitive by harvesting prediction errors. Above it, one-boxers pull ahead decisively and never look back. Adaptive participants typically track whichever strategy is winning with a lag — watch their lean percentage drift on the stat strip as rounds accumulate.
